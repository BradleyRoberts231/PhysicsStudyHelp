Kinematic Word Problems

These examples illustrate how to apply the kinematic equations to solve real-world motion problems. Each one is broken down step-by-step and explained like a seasoned physics professor would teach in class, emphasizing both the mathematics and the physical intuition behind each step.

Problem 1: The Speeding Cyclist

Problem: A cyclist accelerates from rest at a constant rate of 2 m/s¬≤ for 6 seconds. How far does the cyclist travel in that time?

  1. Understand the scenario: Starting from rest means the initial velocity \( v_0 = 0 \). The acceleration is constant, so we can use standard kinematic formulas. We're asked to find displacement.
  2. Identify known values: \( a = 2 \ \text{m/s}^2 \), \( t = 6 \ \text{s} \), \( v_0 = 0 \)
  3. Choose the formula: \( \Delta x = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2 \)
  4. Substitute values: \( \Delta x = 0 + \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 \cdot 6^2 = 1 \cdot 36 = 36 \ \text{m} \)
  5. Interpretation: In uniformly accelerated motion, displacement increases with the square of time. Since the cyclist accelerates steadily, they cover 36 meters in 6 seconds.

Problem 2: Falling Apple

Problem: An apple falls from a tree and hits the ground in 1.2 seconds. Assuming no air resistance, how far did it fall?

  1. Physical insight: The apple starts from rest, and gravity is the only force acting on it (\( a = 9.8 \ \text{m/s}^2 \)). Air resistance is negligible.
  2. Knowns: \( v_0 = 0 \), \( a = 9.8 \ \text{m/s}^2 \), \( t = 1.2 \ \text{s} \)
  3. Apply: \( \Delta x = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2 = 0 + 0.5 \cdot 9.8 \cdot 1.44 = 4.9 \cdot 1.44 = 7.06 \ \text{m} \)
  4. Why this matters: Free fall is a foundational example in kinematics. It reinforces the concept of acceleration due to gravity acting on all objects equally.

Problem 3: Braking Car

Problem: A car moving at 20 m/s comes to a stop in 4 seconds. What is its acceleration?

  1. Conceptual analysis: The car is decelerating. Final velocity \( v = 0 \), initial velocity \( v_0 = 20 \), time \( t = 4 \)
  2. Formula: \( v = v_0 + at \Rightarrow 0 = 20 + a \cdot 4 \Rightarrow a = -5 \ \text{m/s}^2 \)
  3. Significance: The negative sign shows the acceleration is in the opposite direction of the initial motion ‚Äî classic deceleration. It‚Äôs a real-world application of Newton‚Äôs laws and motion control.

Problem 4: Displacement with Initial Velocity

Problem: A soccer ball is kicked at 8 m/s and travels with an acceleration of 1.5 m/s¬≤ for 3 seconds. What is its displacement?

  1. Breakdown: Initial velocity \( v_0 = 8 \), \( a = 1.5 \), \( t = 3 \). We want total displacement, so use \( \Delta x = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2 \)
  2. Math: \( \Delta x = 8 \cdot 3 + 0.5 \cdot 1.5 \cdot 9 = 24 + 6.75 = 30.75 \ \text{m} \)
  3. Deeper understanding: This demonstrates the additive nature of motion: the initial velocity contributes linearly, while the acceleration adds a nonlinear (quadratic) component. This is how athletes, vehicles, or projectiles gain range.

Problem 5: Finding Final Velocity

Problem: A sprinter starts at 0 m/s and accelerates at 3.2 m/s¬≤ for 5 seconds. What is their final velocity?

  1. Interpretation: This is straight-line acceleration. We use \( v = v_0 + at \)
  2. Plug in: \( v = 0 + 3.2 \cdot 5 = 16 \ \text{m/s} \)
  3. Takeaway: This example illustrates linear acceleration perfectly ‚Äî acceleration is constant, so velocity increases uniformly with time. This also lets us infer how the athlete's position changes, and forms the basis of velocity-time graphs in physics.
